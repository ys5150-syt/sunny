Landscapes of Overconsumption

How media turns distant waste landscapes into environmental meaning.

Scroll to begin ↓

Why This Matters

Waste is not just a local problem—it’s a global system of inequality. The products we consume daily end up in distant landscapes, burdening communities far from the source of overconsumption.

Fast fashion, e-waste, and disposable goods generate millions of tons of waste annually. Developed nations often export this waste to the Global South, where marginalized communities face the health and environmental consequences.

This is not an individual failure—it’s a structural issue, hidden by distance and media narratives that shape how we see (or ignore) the cost of our consumption.

80% of global e-waste ends up in developing countries, where it’s processed without proper safety measures.

One Place, Many Media Frames

The same waste landscape can be represented in drastically different ways—each frame shapes how we assign responsibility and understand the problem.

Photograph: The Shock of Scale

This photograph of the Atacama Desert landfill emphasizes the sheer scale of fast fashion waste. It uses wide framing to show the vastness of the problem, creating a sense of awe and shock.

What does it show?

The physical volume of waste and its contrast with the desolate landscape.

What does it hide?

The human cost—waste pickers, local communities, and the global supply chain that brought the waste here.

What responsibility does it suggest?

It evokes general guilt but does not specify who is to blame (consumers, corporations, governments).

News Video: Narrated Crisis

A mainstream news segment about the Atacama landfill uses narration, interviews with local officials, and dramatic music to frame the issue as a "growing crisis."

What does it show?

Local "failure" to manage waste, with soundbites from officials about "overwhelming amounts" of imported trash.

What does it hide?

The role of Western fast fashion brands and consumers in producing the waste.

What responsibility does it suggest?

Shifts blame to local governments and "inefficient" waste management systems.

Institutional Report: Infrastructure & Policy

A World Bank report on global waste frames the Atacama landfill as a "development challenge"—focused on infrastructure, policy, and economic solutions.

What does it show?

Data, graphs, and policy recommendations for "sustainable waste management."

What does it hide?

The emotional and human impact of waste on local communities.

What responsibility does it suggest?

Frames waste as a technical problem to be solved by governments and international organizations.

Interview Quote: Local Perspective

An interview with a waste picker in the Atacama landfill offers a counter-narrative to mainstream media.

What does it show?

The daily reality of living with waste—health risks, economic struggle, and frustration with global inequality.

What does it hide?

Nothing—it centers the voice of those most affected, which is often erased in other frames.

What responsibility does it suggest?

Demands accountability from the global systems (brands, consumers, governments) that produce the waste.

Slow Violence

Waste does not arrive all at once. Its harm is slow, invisible, and cumulative—shaping communities over decades.

Pollution seeps into soil and water. Toxins accumulate in bodies. Land becomes uninhabitable. This is slow violence—environmental harm that is not dramatic or immediate, but deadly over time.

Year 1: Waste Arrives

Imported waste begins to pile up. Local communities are told it will be "temporarily stored" while infrastructure is built.

Year 5: Pollution Spreads

Chemicals from e-waste and plastic leach into groundwater. Residents report increased respiratory illnesses and skin conditions.

Year 10: Land is Contaminated

Farmland becomes unusable. Local economies collapse as traditional livelihoods (farming, fishing) are destroyed.

Year 20: Generational Harm

Children born in the area suffer from chronic health issues. The land is permanently damaged—no amount of cleanup can reverse the toxicity.

Emotional / Personal Frame

Focuses on individual stories, empathy, and personal responsibility.

Tone: Compassionate, emotional, personal.
Visual Style: Close-ups, human faces, intimate moments.
Centered: Individual victims and their stories.
Responsibility: Individual consumers (e.g., "Shop less, recycle more").
Audience Reaction: Guilt, empathy, desire to "do something."

Technical / Structural Frame

Focuses on systems, policies, and global inequality.

Tone: Analytical, objective, critical.
Visual Style: Data, maps, graphs, wide shots of systems.
Centered: Corporations, governments, and global supply chains.
Responsibility: Structural change (e.g., policy reform, corporate accountability).
Audience Reaction: Awareness of systemic issues, desire for policy change.

— Professional, 35

— Community organizer, 42

— Undergraduate student, 21

"No, the media made it feel like a problem for ‘other people’—local governments or communities. It didn’t connect to my daily choices."

— Professional, 35

"It made me feel responsible as a global citizen—not just for my own waste, but for demanding change from the systems that allow this."

— Community organizer, 42

"Absolutely a system problem. The media’s focus on individual responsibility distracts from the corporations and governments that profit from waste."

— Community organizer, 42
